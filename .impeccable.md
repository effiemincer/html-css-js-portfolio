## Design Context

### Users
Recruiters and hiring managers across industries, arriving from LinkedIn or direct links. Roughly 50/50 mobile and desktop. They're scanning quickly — deciding within seconds whether to scroll further or bounce. They see dozens of dev portfolios a week, most of which look identical.

### Brand Personality
**Technical, bold, fun.**

Effie is a software developer and AI engineer who builds compilers, 3D renderers, and automation tools. The site should project someone who is deeply competent but not dry — someone with craft *and* personality. The confidence of showing real systems work (Zig compilers, rendering engines) should come through in the design itself, not just the content.

### Aesthetic Direction
- The site must feel **handmade by someone with taste**, not generated. This is the single most important constraint. An AI engineer's portfolio that looks AI-generated is a credibility problem.
- Open to any visual tone — the current warm/orange/bento direction is one option, but the creator is open to radical departures if the result is more distinctive and memorable.
- Theme (light/dark) should be chosen based on what best serves the "technical, bold, fun" personality and recruiter viewing context.
- Anti-references: any portfolio that looks like it came from a template, a Tailwind starter, or a ChatGPT prompt. The generic card grid + gradient hero + "Hi, I'm [Name]" pattern is the enemy.

### Design Principles

1. **Taste is the portfolio.** For someone who works with AI, the design itself is a signal. Every choice — font, color, layout, spacing — should look like a human with opinions made it. No safe defaults.

2. **Bold enough to remember.** Recruiters see hundreds of these. One unusual layout decision, one unexpected color, one moment of delight is worth more than ten well-executed cliches.

3. **Technical without being cold.** Show precision through details (tight spacing, considered typography, clean grids) but keep warmth through personality (unexpected touches, playful moments, human voice).

4. **Earn every element.** If a visual element doesn't strengthen the message or create delight, cut it. No decorative filler. No "looks designy" additions that don't serve a purpose.

5. **Mobile is half the audience.** Every design decision must work at 375px. Not "degrade gracefully" — actually work well. Recruiters on phones are making snap judgments too.
